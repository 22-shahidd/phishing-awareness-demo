🔗 URL Analysis

One of the easiest ways to spot a phishing attack is by carefully reading the URL. Attackers use clever tricks to make fake URLs look real. Here's how to spot them.

1. Typosquatting

Attackers register domains with small typos hoping users won't notice the difference.

✅ Real https://www.google.com
❌ Fake https://www.g00gle.com
✅ Real https://www.paypal.com
❌ Fake https://www.paypa1.com

2. Subdomain Tricks

Attackers put the real brand name as a subdomain to confuse users. The actual domain is something else entirely.

✅ Real https://sbi.co.in
❌ Fake https://sbi.co.in.login-secure.com
Tip: Always read the URL from right to left — the real domain is just before the first single slash (/). In the fake URL above, the real domain is login-secure.com — not sbi.co.in.

3. HTTPS Does Not Mean Safe

Many people think a padlock icon means a site is safe. That is wrong. HTTPS only means the connection is encrypted — it says nothing about whether the site is legitimate or malicious.

⚠️ Still Fake https://secure-bank-login.phish.net
Remember: A phishing site CAN have HTTPS and a padlock. Always verify the domain name itself — not just the padlock.

4. URL Shorteners

Attackers use URL shorteners like bit.ly to hide the real destination of a malicious link.

⚠️ Hidden https://bit.ly/3xKp9mQ
Tip: Before clicking a shortened URL, use a URL expander like checkshorturl.com to see the real destination first.

✅ How To Read a URL Safely

  • Read the domain from right to left — real domain is just before the first /
  • Check for number substitutions — 0 for o, 1 for l, 3 for e
  • Be suspicious of extra words like "secure", "login", "verify" in the domain
  • Expand shortened URLs before clicking
  • HTTPS padlock does not guarantee the site is legitimate
