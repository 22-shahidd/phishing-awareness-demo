🎭 Phishing Techniques

Phishing is not just one attack — it comes in many forms. Here are the most common techniques attackers use today.

🎯 1. Spear Phishing

A highly targeted attack aimed at a specific individual or organization. Unlike generic phishing, spear phishing uses personal information about the victim — their name, job title, colleagues — to make the email look legitimate.

Example: An email appearing to be from your company's CEO asking you to urgently transfer funds or share login credentials.

Who is targeted: Employees, executives, government officials

Why it works: Feels personal and urgent — victim trusts the sender

📱 2. Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing attacks delivered via SMS text messages instead of email. The message usually contains a fake link or asks you to call a number.

Example: "Your SBI account has been suspended. Click here to verify: http://sbi-secure-login.net"

Who is targeted: General public, bank customers

Why it works: People trust SMS more than email and act quickly

📞 3. Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phishing conducted over phone calls. Attackers pretend to be bank officials, tech support, or government agencies to extract sensitive information verbally.

Example: A caller claiming to be from "Microsoft Support" saying your computer has a virus and asking for remote access or payment.

Who is targeted: Elderly people, non-tech-savvy users

Why it works: Human voice feels more trustworthy than text

👥 4. Clone Phishing

An attacker takes a legitimate email that was previously sent — clones it exactly — replaces the real links with malicious ones and resends it from a spoofed address.

Example: You receive what looks like a resent email from your bank with a "Click here to view your statement" button — but the link now points to a fake site.

Who is targeted: Anyone who receives regular emails from banks, services

Why it works: Looks identical to a real email the victim already trusts

🐋 5. Whaling

A type of spear phishing that specifically targets high-profile individuals like CEOs, CFOs, or directors. The goal is usually financial fraud or access to sensitive company data.

Example: A fake legal subpoena email sent to a company's CFO asking for immediate wire transfer or confidential financial records.

Who is targeted: C-level executives, board members

Why it works: Highly researched and crafted — extremely convincing

🌐 6. Pharming

Instead of tricking users with fake links, pharming redirects users from a real website to a fake one by poisoning DNS records or modifying the hosts file on a victim's computer.

Example: You type "www.yourbank.com" in the browser correctly — but due to DNS poisoning you land on a fake lookalike site without realizing it.

Who is targeted: Anyone — no click on a malicious link required

Why it works: Even careful users who type URLs manually can be affected

✅ General Defence Against All Techniques

  • Always verify the sender before acting on any request
  • Never share OTPs, passwords, or card details over call or SMS
  • Use 2FA on all important accounts
  • Check URLs carefully before entering credentials
  • Keep your browser and antivirus updated
  • When in doubt — call the organisation directly on their official number
